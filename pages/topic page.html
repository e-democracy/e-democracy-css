Once again noise battered neighbors came through with flying colors! At the
Airplane Noise meeting last evening, it was truly gratifying to see so many of
our neighbors come forward and stand up for our neighborhoods and our quality
of life. A special shout out to Powderhorn's Cate Long who bundled up her one
year old daughter and came and spoke from the heart all the while bouncing hey
baby on her hip - what a trooper!
I video recorded the FAA's presentation and will post it on YouTube and drop a
note here when it goes up. The presentation was the FAA's response to the
public meeting in October where we complained loudly about the new and
deafening noise over all neighborhoods from Powderhorn to the airport. There
was a lot of information delivered via a PowerPoint presentation that can be
summarized as follows:
a) There are 33% more flights over our neighborhood since the FAA changed
procedures due to a near collision situation that happened in September, 2010.
b) Many of the flights are lower than what we have experienced in the past; the
reason for this remains unknown.
It would be fair to say that we grilled the FAA pretty hard on certain points
they presented. But they are either unable or unwilling to explain why our
noise level has so greatly increased since the near collision incident. It
would seem entirely logical to state that we have substantially more noise
because of the 33% more flights and the lower flights. But my take on this is
the noise has far more to do with the lower elevation the planes are now flying
over us than it does with the number of flights. (We weren't complaining when
the flights were higher, only when they got lower.)
Technically, the FAA stated they do not require the airlines to take off at any
particular angle of ascent - this is left up to the airlines! The FAA sees it's
role as doing all it can to accomodate the needs of the airlines. They have
some sort of guidance that goes something like this: Move the airplanes through
as quickly as possible while doing it safely. There is no consideration given
to the effect of noise on the ground. Please expand on this or correct me if
this is inaccurate.
There is much more to say, those who attended the meeting, please add your
coments or corrections. I'll post some concluding information when I upload the
video to YouTube and post a notice here.
Michael Kehoe

My impressions from the meeting.  I’ll keep it short and sweet but there was a
lot.

These are only my notes and I only speak for myself.   I am only speaking about
the Standish-Ericsson area, so if someone has something to add or change,
please do so responsibly.  I know that emotions are high.

The meeting started at 7pm and ended close to 10:30pm and we were engaged the
entire time.  The FAA presenter was very good.  I encourage you to view his
PowerPoint on the MAC Noise website though I can’t promise it will make sense
without his narrative.  The FAA presentation did confirm our belief that there
are more planes flying north, flying at a lower altitude, all while
concentrated approximately between Cedar and 28th Ave.  That is practically ALL
of Standish and Corcoran.

There was ZERO data on physical noise in the FAA presentation, because the
presentation was not designed to address or research noise issues, only
designed to address and research potential changes in airport and flight
operations.

Even though there was no noise data, the FAA presentation worked in favor of
our neighborhoods.

>From the presentation, we now know the following:
•	Much of this new concentration of flights over our neighborhood is within the
last one year.
•	The airlines control the type of planes they fly, the schedule they fly, the
speed of the plane and other pilot decisions.
•	Pilots will not willingly fly low and slow.  Speed and altitude are their
most important friends.
•	Flight decisions are based on safety, weather, volume of flights in and out
of the airport, and amount of passengers. There are other issues as well, but
those are the main concerns for airport operations.  These decision factors
change daily and the airport, the MAC and the FAA have little to no control
over these issues.
•	Because the airlines control the type of planes flown etc, some days a DC-9
may fly to Denver, the next day a smaller and quieter airplane may fly the same
route.
•	There are less propeller powered planes out of MSP.  This significantly
changes how the faster and more powerful jet powered planes can take-off and
operate.
•	Because of the September 10th near miss, depending on the final destination
planes now must take off from specific runways.
•	There are few exceptions to the runway rules, mostly with really big and
heavy planes like the 777 and 747 to Asia or that damn giant FedEx Airbus 330
that is our neighborhood White Whale (Aye, aye! It was that accursed white
whale that razed me; made a poor pegging lubber of me for ever and a day!....)
•	Because the mix of airplanes has changed, jets can now take off faster and
turn sooner to their destination.  (This is why they are concentrating over our
neighborhood.)
•	The FAA flight instructions given to the airlines and to pilots have
technically not changed in 10 years.  The flight pattern change to our
neighborhood is from the mix of faster aircraft and the (safety first) runways
utilized.

This new mix of largely jet aircraft, now having to use the two runways that
take-off to the north over our neighborhood for safety issues, can take off
faster, and condense their take-off patterns more tightly.  This means
considerably more air traffic over the unmitigated areas surrounding Lake
Nokomis, Lake Hiawatha, Hiawatha Golf Course, 42nd St and all points north.

Those are the details as I remember them.  The meeting was contentious in
parts, repetitive in others, but in my opinion the FAA and the MAC are open to
ideas to address our noise concerns.  We also have some very erudite and
passionate neighbors, maybe they could join SENA?

However, what was shown about airport operations and airplane types, plus a
recovering economy leading to more future flights it’s my opinion this noise is
probably here to stay.  It’s an airport in a city and we live by it – but yes I
am upset that things have changed because like others, I purchased using the
noise contour maps that showed my little slice of SMPLS to be quieter than
others.   But from this meeting, I believe the flights and patterns are
probably here to stay in some current capacity.

My major concern take away from this meeting was a side comment made about how
noise is actually measured. We got a strange answer about how the noise
monitoring stations actually don't impact the noise contour mapping used to
establish mitigation eligible properties, but instead a whole bunch of data
EXCLUSIVE of the noise monitoring stations in our neighborhood!   Holy cow I
hope I am wrong on that one....

We must better understand the noise mapping, because as of right now the MAC is
saying that they will support exploring moving the mitigation lines to the 60
DBL contours, where now that stop is at 65 DBL.  This is very important.

So personally I will be pressing Councilmember Colvin-Roy and Councilmember
Quincy, plus the MAC and the NOC, to change the eligible mitigation contours to
60 DBL so that at least I can have some respite while in my house.  That is
where I see the most value and the most opportunity for me and my family,
though I understand others feel differently.  I do hope others will join me in
pressing for mitigation in concert with other neighbors who will lead the
charge to try to change the flight patterns to their historic routes.

I think it is important to stress that the FAA is concerned with airplane
SAFETY.  They don't tell the airlines what planes to fly, what flights to make,
what airplanes to purchase, or when to fly (in terms of scheduling).  They make
sure that the flights requested have a safe departure...that's all they're
worried about.  If the maximum capacity of outgoing flights at MSP is 100,
they'll let all 100 through...but will stop any above that.  As long as the
requests are below their safety capability standard, they don't care how many
flights go per hour.

The controllers want the planes in the air as soon as possible....and I would
expect the pilots do, too.  I don't know about you, but even if no one is on
the interstate, I still accelerate to the expected rate of travel before
merging into the lane.  I will make a huge assumption here and say I would
expect that pilots "gun it" to get to the required minimum threshold as it's
generally safer in the air than on the ground.

As for requiring a degree of ascent, the FAA doesn't do that.  It is
unreasonable to assume that the same type of plane could fly in the exact same
manner with no regard for weather conditions, fuel, passengers, and/or whatever
other factors pilots have to consider.  Furthermore, since we have no
structural factors to consider (mountains, tall buildings) so the FAA doesn't
seem to have a safety protocol based on ascent.  As mentioned last night,
pilots were getting confused because of the specific airport rules and
regulations which is why airports streamlined their requirements for pilots.
(Which I could be bungling up...)

There was plenty of talk about "moving the track."  Why don't pilots just turn
later?  This is something we need to discuss with our council members.  The
pilots are given the okay by the FAA to turn north over our neighbourhoods when
they reach that ascent threshold minimum.  From where I sit, our problem is
that planes are accelerating just fine which allows them to cross over our
neighbourhoods quicker than they have in the past.

With regards to fanning: the FAA doesn't send planes over certain
neighbourhoods.  The protocol seems to be "hit your first ascent threshold and
turn north"...there is no fanning.  By sending planes parallel on a northbound
course, the planes are safer.  Thus, if we have 35 planes taking off from the
north runway that generally have the same ascent capabilities, they'll all be
headed north over the same line.

With all this said, the FAA is only concerned about PLANE safety.  Therefore,
if we want to incite some kind of change we need to speak with our council
members who deal directly with NOC....who, based on last night's meeting...are
WAITING for a recommendation from Minneapolis residents.  Please keep this in
mind when we go to these meetings guns-blazin' - it is prudent to remain
somewhat calm in the face of adversity.  We have some great people working on
our behalf and thus far our questions have been answered in a fair and
satisfactory manner.

That said, I want to thank everyone who attended.  I appreciated the input and
was glad we didn't have to resort to name calling and derision.

Have you called your council member today?  :p

Many key points from Tuesday’s NOC Public Input meeting have already been made,
thank you to all who have posted.  I would like to add my perspective and
perhaps clarify a few things.  The FAA report confirmed that what we have said
is true: there are more flights, flying at lower altitudes, leaving the runway
sooner and turning earlier.  The intervals between flights are also shorter.
The analysis and report came as a response to a formal inquiry from the City.
We believe that establishing these facts is critical to our ability to confront
this issue. Now, no one can deny these facts.

I want to clarify what happened at the Noise Oversight Committee meeting last
week. The committee was being asked to make some decisions related to the
airport’s 2020 improvements plan.  The plan makes predictions about the numbers
of passengers and potential changes at the airport and then predicts the
impacts on area residents (including noise) in the form of noise contours.  The
committee was asked to approve or deny a sound mitigation plan for certain
Minneapolis homes based on these projections.  CM Quincy and I argued that the
vote was premature because we do not yet know what homes will need mitigation.
Here’s why: We know that MSP wants to implement new performance based
navigation (PBN) practices and that the FAA will present proposed new flight
tracks in March.  We have not taken a position for or against.  But if those
tracks are going to change where the planes are flying, then we do not know
what the noise impacts will look like in 2020.  I argued strenuously that we
need to know where the planes will really be flying before establishing a map
for sound mitigation but the committee voted to tell MAC to go ahead with the
sound mitigation plan based on a map already produce.

We are concerned that by agreeing to the specific contours on the map we would
foreclose the opportunity for mitigation in areas affected by different
procedures in the future.  We documented our objections and will continue this
fight on to the MAC.

Regarding the 60 dnl (as mentioned by Kyle). The city took actions to formally
establish 60 dnl as the standard in our community for sound mitigation.  Our
approach of asking MAC to use the locally adopted community standard (noise
mitigation to the 60 dnl contour) succeeded. It is a big victory that MAC staff
used our local standard in preparing the map they are recommending and which
the NOC approved as an advisory to the MAC.  I appreciate that and acknowledged
on Tuesday night that it is a huge move, beyond what any other airport in the
country has done.

Tuesday night, the FAA and MAC said they are waiting for the City’s input about
where planes should go. While Carl Rydeen of FAA made an offer that we are
following up, it was not very clear what was being offered. Another issue is
that we have not seen any proposed PBN procedures yet – they will be created by
the FAA, not the City - and they could impact everything. The FAA is scheduled
to present on RNAV to the NOC in March.  Federal and state requirements, as
well as plain old decency, require a process of review for those proposals–
with a public hearing and a separate EA.  This is not simply in the hands of
Minneapolis.  A careful evaluation of these proposed procedures and corridors
will be needed and you will be engaged in that process (as well as residents of
surrounding cities.)

Now that we are armed with facts about what is going on above our
neighborhoods, we are exploring what can improve the situation.  Some things we
would ask for include not allowing planes to leave the runway early, not
allowing early turns, requiring planes to climb to a certain altitude above our
homes and lengthening the intervals between take-offs.  Carl Rydeen has stated
that none of these changes are possible due to FAA regulations.  If Mr. Rydeen
is constrained by FAA rules, then we will fight to change the rules.

The city has been very aggressively working on this issue since it first came
to our attention last summer.  I personally devote a lot of time to this
because it is a high priority. Please be assured - I’m fighting for you.
Sandy Colvin Roy

First, we need to get to the bottom of what has caused the airplane noise in
our neighborhoods to increase so dramatically. Tuesday's report by FAA did not
answer this, it was a "departure analysis" not a noise analysis.

In the PowerPoint presentation, a lot of time was spent talking about how the
"fleet mix" has changed resulting in more jets (with the implication being more
noise). The change in fleet mix likely took place over a number of years - not
overnight.

Remember this fact: before the near-accident in Sept., 2010, the noise was
tolerable; after FAA made the changes to the take off track, during winter,
2011 - the noise was intolerable. At the same time we all noticed the airplanes
were taking off much, much lower over our homes than ever before.

FAA has answered one issue with the statement that we now have 33% more flights
over our neighborhoods than before the near-miss. Carl Rydeen (FAA's top
administrator at MSP) said repeatedly over a number of meetings that he has no
clue on why we're reporting that the airplanes are flying lower overhead. He
says repeatedly that he is certain that the pilots are climbing at their
maximum safe rate on takeoff, but he has only his opinion to back that up - no
facts.

This is the CRITICAL missing piece: the planes are flying lower over our homes
and nobody will explain why that is. Until this is answered conclusively,
nothing else matters.

Make a mental note of this: Per Jim Spensley, SMAAC, when an airplane lowers
its altitude by 50%, the "Sound Intensity" on the ground increases by 8 times!

Is there a reason why the airlines might prefer to take off at a more gradual
rate? Does it save fuel? Did the airlines use the near-miss incident to change
their take-off procedures and used the FAA's change in flight tracks to be
their cover?

With all due respect to airline, FAA, MAC, and NOC employees, they need FACTS
to back up their arguments - not casual opinions. It's not enough to say of the
pilots, "I know these guys, I'm sure they're getting up there as fast as they
can." That's unprofessional; I want facts.

Sound Intensity:
http://adg.stanford.edu/aa241/noise/noise.html

Thanks for your enthusiasm Michael!

I believe that the earlier right-turn (north turn) is a primary reason the
airplanes to be lower when they turn north, lower when they fly over our
houses, and further east than in the past. I think focusing on shifting the
"Track" back to the west should be one of our strongest demands. Aircraft
should stay on the heading of the 30R runway (northwesterly direction) for a
longer period before turning right/north. This offers three benefits:

1.) Aircraft fly over more airport-owned and non-residential property for a
longer period of time
2.) Aircraft fly over MAC-mitigated properties and less over non-mitigated
properties (the contours on the DNL noise map extend for miles to the
northwest, and only a short distance straight north). We would still hear
aircraft, but it would be more bearable, more like in years before.
3.) Aircraft would be flying at a higher altitude when flying over homes,
leading to a SIGNIFICANT decrease in noise pollution (as you mention)

Next steps:
A.) Meet as concerned, motivated neighbors (reply if you are interested)
B.) Request a meeting with our City Council Members to bring requests through
them to the Noise Oversight Committee, as we have been invited to do.

To our Council Members: please consider scheduling a public meeting of people
in Standish-Ericsson, Powderhorn, Phillips, Corcoran, Bancroft and other
newly-affected neighborhoods. I request to be a part of any such meeting.

Thanks!
